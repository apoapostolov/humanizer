# Humanization pattern catalog

Use this catalog when a draft needs systematic diagnosis. It supports the editing
sequence in [humanizing-text.md](humanizing-text.md); it does not replace that
sequence.

Patterns are contextual prompts, not proof of AI authorship. Diagnose clusters and
reader impact. Preserve intentional rhetoric, genre conventions, technical terms,
quoted language, and the writer's established voice. Do not score or rewrite
quoted examples, code blocks, or attributed excerpts unless the brief includes
them.

## Words and claims: patterns 1-6

### 1. Significance inflation

**Tell:** Ordinary details are called pivotal, profound, timeless, legendary,
transformative, or representative of a broader shift.

**Repair:** Name the concrete change and its consequence.

### 2. Genre-register drift

**Tell:** Stock genre language replaces the vocabulary of the actual world,
profession, publication, or speaker.

**Repair:** Use named practices, materials, institutions, tools, and stakes.

### 3. Corporate and motivational register

**Tell:** Words such as *impactful*, *empower*, *leverage*, *optimize*,
*meaningful*, *deeply*, or *genuinely* replace a specific action or result.

**Repair:** State who did what and what changed.

### 4. Imported psychological vocabulary

**Tell:** A narrator or character uses therapeutic or diagnostic language that
does not fit the speaker, period, genre, or evidence.

**Repair:** Render observed behavior, sensation, memory, and choice. Keep exact
clinical terms when the context requires them.

### 5. Promotional language

**Tell:** Neutral prose sounds like a brochure through words such as *vibrant*,
*renowned*, *stunning*, *groundbreaking*, *thriving*, or *world-class*.

**Repair:** Replace praise with a fact the reader can evaluate.

### 6. Abstract noun preference

**Tell:** *Community*, *resilience*, *innovation*, *culture*, *hope*,
*transformation*, or *impact* carries the sentence without a visible act or object.

**Repair:** Show the exchange, decision, object, or outcome that supports the
abstraction.

## Sentence movement: patterns 7-16

### 7. Em-dash cluster

**Tell:** Dashes manufacture drama, rhythm, or parenthetical depth several times
in one passage.

**Repair:** Keep a dash for a real interruption or turn. Use a period, comma,
colon, or reordered sentence elsewhere.

### 8. Rule-of-three padding

**Tell:** Claims, adjectives, or examples arrive in polished triads because three
sounds complete.

**Repair:** Keep the number of points the content earns. One precise detail often
beats three generic ones.

### 9. Negative-then-positive clarification

**Tell:** Repeated *It is not X. It is Y.* and *X rather than Y* constructions
manufacture emphasis or sophistication without sharpening the distinction.

**Repair:** State the claim directly or make the contrast factual.

### 10. Copula avoidance

**Tell:** *Serves as*, *stands as*, *functions as*, *represents*, *boasts*, or
*offers* replaces *is* or *has*.

**Repair:** Prefer the plain verb unless the substitute adds meaning.

### 11. Elegant variation

**Tell:** Synonyms rotate to avoid repeating the correct name, technical term, or
role.

**Repair:** Repeat the right word. Consistency is clearer than decorative
variation.

### 12. Hedging stack

**Tell:** *Perhaps*, *possibly*, *arguably*, *somewhat*, *in some ways*, and *to
an extent* accumulate until the sentence makes no usable claim.

**Repair:** Keep only uncertainty supported by the evidence.

### 13. Gerund opening

**Tell:** Paragraphs start with static retrospective phrases such as *Having
worked on...*, *Knowing that...*, or *Feeling the weight...*.

**Repair:** Open on the action, observation, or claim that moves the passage.

### 14. Balanced compound conclusion

**Tell:** A paragraph ends on a polished balance such as challenge and
opportunity, fear and hope, or old and new.

**Repair:** Land on the fact, consequence, image, or next decision.

### 15. Trailing qualifier

**Tell:** A sentence gives final emphasis to *for the most part*, *as best they
could*, *to a degree*, or *where possible*.

**Repair:** Move necessary qualification earlier or end on the hard fact.

### 16. Not-just-X-but-Y construction

**Tell:** *Not just X, but Y* repeatedly signals nuance or escalation without
adding evidence.

**Repair:** State the stronger claim directly and support it.

## Paragraphs and point of view: patterns 17-27

### 17. Topic-sentence paragraph

**Tell:** Every paragraph announces its subject, supplies examples, and closes
with a summary.

**Repair:** Start where the thought becomes useful and stop when it has done its
work.

### 18. Continuity bridge sentence

**Tell:** *Furthermore*, *building on this*, *against this backdrop*, or *with
that in mind* exists only to connect neighboring paragraphs.

**Repair:** Cut the bridge. Reorder the paragraphs if the connection remains
unclear.

### 19. Thematic restatement ending

**Tell:** The final sentence explains what the preceding scene or passage meant.

**Repair:** End on the evidence and trust the reader to infer the theme.

### 20. Even-rhythm paragraph

**Tell:** Sentences share the same length, cadence, and grammatical shape.

**Repair:** Let sentence length follow thought. Use short sentences for hard
turns and longer ones for connected reasoning.

### 21. Signposting sentence

**Tell:** *It is important to note*, *the key point is*, *here is what matters*,
or *let's dive in* announces emphasis before delivering content.

**Repair:** Begin with the important content.

### 22. Backstory dump on first mention

**Tell:** A person, place, product, or institution arrives with a complete
history before the present action can continue.

**Repair:** Introduce what matters now. Reveal history when it changes the
reader's understanding or a character's choice.

### 23. Emotion announcement

**Tell:** The narrator names emotion instead of rendering what a person notices,
does, or cannot do.

**Repair:** Use bodily response, attention, gesture, and choice when useful. Keep
a direct emotion word when blunt self-naming suits the voice.

### 24. Sympathetic narrator insert

**Tell:** *Little did she know*, *fate had other plans*, or *things were about to
change* reassures, foreshadows, or explains from outside the scene.

**Repair:** Remove the intrusion or foreshadow through a concrete detail.

### 25. Over-explained motivation

**Tell:** The narrator supplies a full reason for each action rather than letting
behavior, context, or prior events carry it.

**Repair:** Show the choice and enough evidence for the reader to infer why.

### 26. Analytical self-awareness

**Tell:** A first-person narrator perfectly diagnoses their own defenses,
projections, motives, and emotional substitutions.

**Repair:** Match the speaker's plausible self-knowledge. Let gaps and
contradictions remain when they belong to the voice.

### 27. Explanatory memory flashback

**Tell:** A memory arrives exactly when needed to explain the present emotion or
deliver backstory.

**Repair:** Keep memory fragmentary and triggered by a specific sensation,
object, phrase, or action.

## Dialogue and setting: patterns 28-38

### 28. Expressive dialogue tag

**Tell:** A tag adds a clause such as *her voice heavy with sorrow* or *his words
carrying authority*.

**Repair:** Use *said*, *asked*, no tag, or an action beat. Put emotion in the line
and situation.

### 29. Dialogue that explains shared backstory

**Tell:** Characters tell each other facts they both know so the reader can catch
up.

**Repair:** Give the question to someone who lacks the information, or reveal it
through disagreement, action, or consequence.

### 30. Perfect grammar in all speech

**Tell:** Every speaker uses complete, polished sentences and the same register.

**Repair:** Distinguish speakers through cadence, vocabulary, omission, and what
each assumes. Avoid cartoon dialect and random errors.

### 31. Dialogue tags used for emotion

**Tell:** Characters constantly hiss, bark, scoff, groan, exclaim, or stammer.

**Repair:** Use a marked tag only when the sound or act matters. Otherwise use
*said* or an action beat.

### 32. Character who summarizes for the reader

**Tell:** A character recaps the plot, argument, or problem in a polished list.

**Repair:** Let the reader assemble the situation. If a recap is necessary, make
it serve a real action.

### 33. Generic atmospheric opener

**Tell:** A scene starts with free-floating weather, darkness, silence, or mood.

**Repair:** Anchor atmosphere to a body, tool, task, or decision.

### 34. Over-complete information

**Tell:** The draft answers every likely question and inventories every aspect of
a place, system, or person at first mention.

**Repair:** Select what matters now. Add the rest when it changes a decision.

### 35. Culture or belief as flavor

**Tell:** Religion, ritual, identity, or custom appears as decoration without
obligation, authority, practice, disagreement, or cost.

**Repair:** Show what people do, who expects it, who pays, and what happens when
someone refuses.

### 36. Monster as obstacle

**Tell:** Genre fiction or game writing treats a creature only as content to
defeat.

**Repair:** Give it an ecology, appetite, bargain, history, limit, or competing
interest when the world needs that depth.

### 37. Setting as protagonist service

**Tell:** A place or community exists mainly to offer clues, opportunities, or
validation to the protagonist.

**Repair:** Give the setting pressures and routines that continue without the
protagonist.

### 38. Problem framed as content invitation

**Tell:** *This presents an opportunity*, *the party may investigate*, or *the
reader can explore* turns a concrete problem into a packaged activity.

**Repair:** Present the situation, actors, stakes, and worsening pressure. Let the
reader or character choose whether to engage.

## Openings and endings: patterns 39-43

### 39. Sentimental resolution

**Tell:** A difficult passage ends with an unearned warm gesture, reassurance, or
claim that everyone will endure.

**Repair:** Let the consequence remain. Use warmth only when a specific action
earned it.

### 40. Editorial narrative close

**Tell:** *And so began...*, *history would remember...*, *the night kept its
secrets*, or *an uncertain future awaited* steps outside the passage to close it.

**Repair:** End on the last action, fact, image, or decision.

### 41. Repeated weather openings

**Tell:** Every scene begins with rain, cold, dawn, darkness, or a lowering sky.

**Repair:** Vary openings among action, dialogue, sound, object, interruption,
claim, and physically experienced weather.

### 42. Parallel opening-and-ending echo

**Tell:** A scene opens and closes on the same image or phrase to create automatic
symmetry.

**Repair:** Let the ending follow what changed. Keep an echo when transformation
of that image is the scene's real structure.

### 43. Lesson stated directly

**Tell:** The close says what the character learned or what the audience should
understand.

**Repair:** Cut the lesson. Strengthen the preceding evidence if the meaning
disappears without it.

## Lists and compression: patterns 44-55

### 44. Flat comma chain

**Tell:** Four or more equal-looking units create the appearance of detail without
rank, sequence, or pressure.

**Repair:** Choose the decisive detail, build a sequence, or use a scannable list
when every member matters.

### 45. Adjective barrage

**Tell:** Several labels precede one noun while none identifies a useful material,
condition, scale, ownership, or function.

**Repair:** Keep modifiers that change the reader's image or decision. Put
atmosphere in what the thing does.

### 46. Pseudo-inventory

**Tell:** A room, product, setting, or plan lists plausible components merely to
prove completeness.

**Repair:** Name an item when its condition, owner, use, cost, or absence matters.
Use a real list for a real inventory.

### 47. Category drift

**Tell:** One series treats people, objects, abstractions, causes, and outcomes as
equivalent members.

**Repair:** Separate conceptual kinds. State the material event, then its social
or conceptual consequence.

### 48. Unordered causal pile

**Tell:** Causes or results appear without order, weight, dependency, or a point
where someone can intervene.

**Repair:** Build the chain: cause, response, consequence, current pressure.

### 49. Example dump

**Tell:** A long run of examples substitutes for a rule or representative case.

**Repair:** State the principle and use one revealing example. Group necessary
examples by function.

### 50. Headless modifier train

**Tell:** Multiple qualifications arrive before the reader reaches the noun or
main action.

**Repair:** Put the noun and verb early. Move history, source, and exceptions into
later clauses or sentences.

### 51. Serial verb chain

**Tell:** A character watches, listens, considers, remembers, realizes, and
decides while no visible action changes the scene.

**Repair:** Show the clue and the resulting action. Keep a fast chain when speed
is the point and the last verb changes the situation.

### 52. Decorative and-chain

**Tell:** Repeated *and* lengthens a list without creating labor, panic, ritual,
breath, or speaker-specific rhythm.

**Repair:** Break the sequence into stages and end on failure or decision. Keep
the chain only when its breath pattern does useful work.

### 53. Parenthetical list inflation

**Tell:** A parenthesis, bracket, or dash hides a second catalog after the main
sentence has made its point.

**Repair:** Keep a short necessary example or exception. Give larger sets their
own sentence, list, or table.

### 54. List as paragraph substitute

**Tell:** One sentence or bullet contains several independent procedures,
conditions, outcomes, and exceptions.

**Repair:** Separate trigger, action, result, and exception. Use numbered steps
for real sequences.

### 55. False completeness

**Tell:** *All*, *every*, *comprehensive*, *many aspects*, or an open-ended list
claims mastery the passage does not provide.

**Repair:** State the actual scope. Use *all* only for a closed, defined set.

## Secondary cleanup patterns

Use these patterns after the first cleanup pass. They are common ways a rewrite
can remain artificial even after the obvious vocabulary has gone.

- **Cleanup-pass uniformity:** Every paragraph ends with a quotable one-liner,
  balanced antithesis, or run of short declarative fragments. Keep only emphasis
  the argument earns; combine manufactured punchlines and let some sentences end
  without a flourish.
- **Aphorism formula:** Constructions such as *X is the language of Y*, *X is the
  currency of Y*, or *X is not a tool but a mirror* make an ordinary claim sound
  profound. Replace the formula with the concrete relationship.
- **Fake-candid opener:** *Honestly?*, *Here's the thing*, *Let's be real*, or a
  rhetorical question creates theatrical intimacy before a routine point. Start
  with the point unless the opener belongs to the writer's demonstrated voice.
- **Replacement tic:** A removed cliché is replaced everywhere by the same new
  bridge or landing phrase, such as *the real question* or *what this means in
  practice*. The usual repair is omission, not a standard substitute.
- **Diff-anchored writing:** Documentation or comments narrate that something
  *was added*, *was changed*, or *replaces the old approach* when the document is
  not a changelog or migration guide. Describe the current behavior and keep
  history only when readers need it to act.
- **Speculative gap filling:** Missing information becomes stock biography or
  motive: someone *keeps a low profile*, *values privacy*, or *likely* followed a
  plausible path. State the gap once or omit it; do not turn absence into fact.
- **Speculative scenario opener:** *Imagine a world where*, *picture a future in
  which*, or similar framing lists desirable outcomes instead of supporting a
  claim. State the proposition and evidence directly. Preserve fiction, useful
  thought experiments, and concrete instructional examples.
- **Novelty inflation:** *Coined*, *introduced*, *the idea nobody is naming*, or
  an undefined invented label makes an established concept sound newly
  discovered. Verify a real priority claim; otherwise describe the application
  or mechanism without claiming invention.
- **Generic endorsement closer:** *This is worth your time*, *bookmark this*,
  *don't miss this*, or *thank me later* performs enthusiasm without explaining
  the recommendation. Name the relevant value and audience or remove the
  sign-off.
- **Evidence-strength drift:** Verbs such as *proves*, *demonstrates*, or
  *confirms*, unsupported thresholds, and citation dumps claim more than the
  evidence carries. Match the verb and scope to the evidence, name the baseline,
  and explain the relevant source.

## Delivery and provenance

Check these publication-level patterns after the prose itself is sound:

- **Superficial `-ing` analysis:** Remove participial tails that claim to
  highlight, underscore, showcase, foster, or ensure something without evidence.
- **Vague attribution:** Name the source behind *experts say*, *critics argue*, or
  *reports suggest*, or remove the borrowed authority.
- **Notability roll call:** Replace outlet names, follower counts, and prestige
  claims with the relevant statement, work, or event.
- **Formulaic challenges and prospects:** Replace generic obstacles, resilience,
  and outlook sections with dated facts, decisions, and uncertainty.
- **Passive voice when agency matters:** Name the actor when responsibility,
  causation, or sequence matters. Keep passive voice when the actor is unknown,
  irrelevant, or deliberately backgrounded.
- **False range:** Replace dramatic *from X to Y* constructions unless the
  endpoints define a real spectrum.
- **Mechanical formatting:** Remove decorative bold labels, title case, emoji,
  repeated label-colon lists, unnecessary small tables, skipped heading levels,
  and thematic breaks that merely precede headings. Keep hierarchy and tables
  that help readers scan or compare.
- **Typographic over-polish:** Match surrounding quotation marks, punctuation,
  capitalization, and spacing.
- **Assistant residue:** Remove pleasantries, refusals, offers, knowledge-cutoff
  disclaimers, response announcements, prompt restatements, sycophantic praise,
  recap-flattery, exposed reasoning scaffolds, and fake quotations. Keep a brief,
  earned thank-you when the exchange calls for one.
- **Tool fingerprint residue:** Remove internal citation or attachment tokens and
  AI-tool tracking parameters from URLs. Replace a meaningful token with a real,
  verifiable citation; keep the clean destination URL when the link still helps.
- **Template residue:** Resolve placeholders, bracketed instructions, sample
  dates, subject-line scaffolding, unfinished lists, and abrupt cutoffs.
- **Conversational block shape:** In chat, issue comments, direct messages, and
  casual email, break distinct thoughts where the surrounding conversation would.
  Do not impose short-paragraph quotas on formal prose.
- **Voice discontinuity:** Match surrounding register, dialect, spelling, polish,
  and point of view.
- **Fragmented headers:** Delete a one-line restatement beneath a heading when the
  following paragraph already contains the substance.
- **Hyphenated modifier pile:** Rewrite clusters such as *high-quality,
  data-driven, client-facing* instead of mechanically deleting valid hyphens.
- **Audit-report inflation:** Do not attach an exhaustive narrative of minor edits
  unless the user needs an audit trail. Report only consequential choices,
  evidence gaps, and changes the writer may want to reverse.

## How to use the catalog

1. Mark only patterns that materially weaken the draft.
2. Group them by editing layer instead of reporting every local occurrence.
3. Fix meaning and evidence before rhythm and polish.
4. Repair selection and relationships before swapping synonyms.
5. Re-read for voice continuity and publication residue.
6. Stop when the passage is clear, credible, and fit for purpose.
